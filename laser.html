Laser Cutting and Woodshop

20 - 27 September 2018


This week, we used the laser engraver to create four different projects. We created clocks, press fit kits, a choice project, and water bottles. As with the printers last week, we used CorelDRAW to create our projects. The most important thing you will learn from my projects this week, if you had not already picked up on this from previous weeks, is that I am a nerd.



Assignment 1: Clock

We created clocks to be hung on the wall of the Makerspace. The only requirements were that it be original and compatible with a specific clock kit we had.



Because I love to listen to 80s music, and one of my favorite 80s bands is Journey, I decided to try to make a clock out of one of their most iconic album covers, Escape. Because I was not especially familiar with CorelDRAW's Trace Bitmap tool when I first started on this project, I had to restart several times before I finally figured out exactly how to make my clock look like I wanted it to.



Before I put my image into CorelDRAW, I used another application to convert it to grayscale, then I put that grayscale image into CorelDRAW and traced the bitmap. From there, I had to go through and decide which parts of the bitmap I actually wanted to engrave and which parts I wanted to remove. I decided to remove all the letters and background, leaving only the iconic orb and scarab beetle in my clock. I also didn't want my clock to be super dark everywhere, so I removed as much of the background as I could. This took a lot of time, because I had to go through every one of the almost 300 "curves," as CorelDRAW labels them, in the bitmap of my image. Once I accomplished that, though, I was ready to laser engrave.



I am very satisfied with how my clock turned out. Because of my inexperience with CorelDRAW, I honestly did not expect my clock to turn out so well, so when I saw the final product and how much it really looked like my original image, despite the background being edited out, I was very pleased. If I were to do this project again, I would not change anything. I would just be faster in creating my clock, because of all my new experience with CorelDRAW.

Assignment 2: Press Fit Kits


"What is a press fit kit?" you ask. "Well," I tell you, "it is a collection of shapes that fit together by means of slits and which do not fall apart because those slits are tight enough that friction holds the pieces in place.
We had to create press fit kits out of at least three different shapes. Because I am a nerd, I created a kit with 6 different shapes and 9 different pieces and I named it the Fellowship of the Shapes.



Creating these shapes was pretty easy. I made the 9 shapes on CorelDRAW, each approximately the same size, with my hexagon and pentagons slightly smaller because they represented my dwarf and my hobbits. I used a caliper to measure the thickness of my cardboard, and then created slits in my shapes of that width, so that my shapes would fit together.



Finally, I found grayscale images of my 9 characters, converted them to bitmaps using CorelDRAW, and put them on their respective shapes. from there, I was ready to print.




My shapes turned out very well. They all stuck together correctly, and I was very proud of my nerdiness when I was finished. The only thing I would change would be that some of my images turned out strangely because my images were so dark that the laser cut through the top layer of cardboard, which made my image look very strange, but other than brightening up some of my images to solve that problem, there is nothing I would change if I were to make this again.

Assignment 3: Living Hinge


Of the several options given for this part of the assignment, I found living hinges the most interesting, so I decided to try to make one. A living hinge is simply a piece of material with enough small cuts in it to theoretically not make the material fall apart but to still allow it to flex freely. I decided to just create a geometric pattern which would divide my material into small pieces, and then use CorelDRAW's virtual segment delete tool to remove small pieces of each of those cuts to hold the entire piece together.




My living hinge turned out very well. There were a few pieces that fell out, but that was only because, since my piece was so large, I did not use the virtual segment delete tool on all the pieces because I missed several of them. However, the piece flexes very well and has yet to fall apart. I am very pleased with the results.

Assignment 4: Water Bottle


For our final assignment this week, we had to use the rotary attachment for the laser cutter to engrave either a water bottle or a glass. This project was different from the others because, if we wanted to put images, the laser cutter could only do two colors because instead of cutting through various depths to create various shades, as the laser could do on wood or cardboard, on the water bottle, the laser could only cut away the paint on top of the stainless steel. Because of this, the only colors available to me were the blue of the water bottle's paint and the silver of the stainless steel. Thus, instead of putting NASA's iconic "Meatball" logo on my water bottle, which would have required more than those two colors, I put NASA's simpler "Worm" logo on my water bottle, as well as a quote from Gene Kranz, one of NASA's best-known and most inspirational flight directors.




I am very happy with how my water bottle turned out. I would change absolutely nothing. The blue goes perfectly with my theme because, although I did not use the Meatball logo in my water bottle, the blue of the water bottle is very similar to the blue of the Meatball logo. The words turned out very clear on my bottle, and the whole piece is very aesthetically pleasing.



In this file, you will notice that the words are rotated 90 degrees counter-clockwise. This is simply because of the orientation of the rotary attachment and water bottle in the laser engraver.
